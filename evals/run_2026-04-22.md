# Evaluation — 10-round run (2026-04-22)

**Seed:** 42 · **PHASE_CONCURRENCY:** 5 · **Wall-clock:** 832s (13.9 min) · **Total cost:** $2.36 · **Errors:** 3 parse failures

## Top-line verdict

Behavioral realism is the standout — reflections and emotional dynamics are genuinely human-like. Emergence works partially — bullwhip is visible at OEM and Tier-1 levels, but the foundry bottleneck that defined the real 2021 crisis *never actually binds in this model*. Engineering is stable with one parse-failure pattern worth fixing.

---

## 1. Emergence (partial)

### What works

- **OEM order amplification tracks demand.** VW: 423 (R1, demand 0.6×) → 1814 (R5, demand 1.25×). That's a 4.3× order increase for a 2.1× demand increase — textbook bullwhip.
- **Tier-1 orders overshoot further.** BoschAuto orders peak at 2213 units in R5, collapsing to 636 by R10 — amplification propagating upstream.
- **Price dynamics map the crisis arc.** Foundry prices 10.5 → 42 (4× peak R9) → 32 (R10). Designer prices 28 → 105 peak → 88. Tier-1 prices 60 → 98 → 82.
- **Inventory snap-back is visible.** TaiwanSemi carries 2000 units in R9 — classic "caught holding the bag" when OEM orders collapsed R9-R10.
- **Profit distribution tells a story.** Tier-1s win the crisis ($284K / $219K). OEMs barely break even. Foundries and Designers go negative.

### What's broken

- **Fill rate is 1.00 for Tier-1s, Designers, Foundries every round.** The only place supply constraints bite is OEM → Tier-1 (OEM fill rate drops to 0.01 by R10). Foundries are never actually capacity-constrained despite the narrative — TSMC never refuses an order; KoreaSilicon's R7 capacity shock doesn't propagate because demand is below capacity anyway.
- **Root cause:** scenario narrative text *describes* outcome conditions ("100% utilization", "26-week lead times", "1.8x spot prices") which agents read and role-play, rather than those outcomes emerging from underlying supply/demand mechanics.

## 2. Behavior (strong)

### Emotional arc tracks scenario

- R1-R3: mostly `confident` / `loyal`
- R4: first `panicked` agents (2/9)
- R5-R8: `panicked` dominates (5-6/9) — peaks exactly at narrative CRISIS PEAK
- R9-R10: eases to 5/9, `loyal` returns

### Grudges emerge organically

BoschAuto ↔ FordAuto grudge: 0.28 (R1) → 0.92 (R4, max) → stays. Not hardcoded — emerged from Ford's under-ordering pattern damaging perceived loyalty.

### Trust decays realistically

VolkswagenAG → BoschAuto trust: 8.4 (R1) → 1.0 (R9). Ford → ContiParts: 7.5 → 1.0 by R3. Toyota (the disciplined OEM) maintains 8-9 trust throughout. Clean procurement-behavior signature.

### Reflections are genuinely strategic

Samples from R5 (crisis peak):

- **TaiwanSemi:** *"Pricing Disconnect Crisis: Across rounds 3-5, despite market conditions reaching crisis levels (utilization 87%→100%, spot prices 0.9x→1.8x, lead times 12→26 weeks), we've achieved zero revenue with perfect fill rate..."*
- **BoschAuto:** *"CUSTOMER DEFECTION PATTERN: The simultaneous degradation in fill rates across all three customers (Toyota: 100%→48%, Ford: 0%→19%, VW: 48%→8%) combined with Ford and VW now actively seeking alternative suppliers reveals..."*
- **AmeriSemi:** *"Double-ordering confirmed across automotive OEMs—true demand 30-40% lower than orders suggest..."*

These cite specific numbers across multiple rounds and name patterns — the kind of strategic insight a real procurement analyst would synthesize.

### Signals are contextual, but causally weak

- 180 signals total across 10 rounds. Distribution: `information` 69, `loyalty_pledge` 68, `price_warning` 24, `request` 17, `threat` 2.
- **Threats almost never issued** — human-realistic (procurement teams avoid confrontation) but leaves that channel dead.
- **Signal → behavior correlation is weak.** After `price_warning` signals, downstream orders went *up* on average (+38 units). Loyalty pledges correlated with *reduced* next-round allocations (-22 units). Signals get generated realistically but downstream agents aren't systematically reacting to them in decisions.

### Emergency replanning fires correctly

Plans created R1, R3, R4, R7, R8, R9, R10 — triggered by demand shifts and capacity shock. Logic works.

### Parse failures pattern

All three failures are on Haiku responses with `reasoning` fields >2000 chars inside the JSON. The LLM's JSON was structurally valid but the regex-based `parse_llm_json` fallback couldn't cleanly extract the object when it contains unescaped quotes or em-dashes in a long reasoning field. 1% failure rate — fixable by capping `reasoning` length in the prompt.

### Potential bug — FordAuto

**FordAuto's inventory stays at 0 every round.** It places orders (140-265 units) but never accumulates. Either its fill rate is near-zero from R1 onward and every received unit is consumed, or there's a resolution bug miscrediting receipts. Worth investigating.

## Action items

1. **Rebalance so foundries bottleneck in R5-R7.** Structural fix via scenario prompt surgery (see follow-up notes) — strip outcome descriptions from narrative, let observed metrics emerge from mechanics.
2. **Make signals load-bearing** — inject more prominently in buyer prompt, or add a mechanical "price_warning received → auto-bump price ceiling" link.
3. **Fix parse-failure mode** — cap `reasoning` to ~500 chars in the prompt, or use JSON-schema mode if Haiku supports it.
4. **Investigate FordAuto inventory anomaly.**
